CampSpark

01 · The Problem

Every summer, the same chaos

Every spring, I found myself drowning in the same annual ritual: dozens of browser tabs open across camp websites, a half-finished spreadsheet, a WhatsApp thread with other parents, and a creeping sense that the good camps were already full.

I'm a working parent with a demanding job. My daughter has specific interests, a specific schedule, and (like most kids) strong opinions about which friends she wants to be with. None of the tools I found helped me solve all of this at once.

"The problem isn't that camp information doesn't exist. It's that it's everywhere, and none of it talks to each other."

The pain wasn't just mine. When I talked to other parents in my network, the pattern was identical: hours of manual research, coordination over text, and decisions made under deadline pressure because registration windows close fast.

  • Camp info scattered across dozens of individual websites and PDFs
  • No way to filter by age, interest, schedule, and budget simultaneously
  • No visibility into which camps friends were attending
  • Coordination happening in WhatsApp groups and spreadsheets
  • Registration anxiety: good camps fill up weeks before most parents start looking
02 · The Insight

This is a coordination problem, not a search problem

Existing solutions treated this as a directory problem: build a better list of camps. But that missed the real issue. Parents don't just need to find camps. They need to decide with incomplete information, coordinate with other parents, and plan across an entire summer at once.

The real job to be done: Give me confidence that my child will have a great summer, with the right activities, the right friends, and no scheduling gaps I'll panic about in June.

The strategic differentiator wasn't better data. It was reducing cognitive load by combining discovery, planning, and social coordination in one place. Something no existing tool did.

I also noticed a macro tailwind: return-to-office mandates were making the summer childcare problem more acute for dual-income families. The pain was getting worse, not better.

03 · What I Built

A prototype my daughter actually used

I built a working desktop-first prototype using Claude Code. No engineering team, no design handoff, no backlog. Just a PM who knew what she wanted to test and built it herself.

The prototype focused on three things:

Feature 01
Smart Filtering
Filter by age, interest, location radius, time window, and budget simultaneously, saving filter presets per child.
Feature 02
Camp Cards
Enough detail to make a decision without opening external tabs. Dates, times, price, location, activity type, and a direct link to register.
Feature 03
Summer Plan View
A consolidated week-by-week view of selected camps across the summer, shareable with other parents by link.
Feature 04
Friend Visibility
See which camps a child's friends are attending, surfacing social signals that matter most to kids (and parents).

I deliberately excluded registration and payment from the MVP. CampSpark is discovery-first. We send parents to camp websites to register. This kept the prototype lightweight and avoided liability without sacrificing core value.

Real validation: My daughter used the prototype to browse and select her summer camps. She filtered by interest, checked which camps her friends were considering, and built her own wish list. Watching an 11-year-old use a tool you built, and having it actually work for her, is a different kind of signal than any survey.

04 · Key Decisions

What I cut, and why

Every zero-to-one project is defined as much by what you don't build as what you do. A few of the harder calls:

Cut
Mobile app
Summer planning is a sit-down activity, not a daily habit. Desktop-first let me focus on the complex filtering and comparison workflows that matter most.
Cut
Registration & payment
Would have required legal, ops, and engineering complexity that would've sunk the MVP. Sending users to camp sites costs nothing and preserves focus.
Kept
Friend coordination
The most complex social feature, but also the most defensible differentiator. No directory does this. It's the reason parents would choose CampSpark over a Google search.
Kept
Curated camp data
Instead of real-time scraping (fragile, legal risk), I curated a local dataset. Good enough to validate the concept, manageable to maintain for a prototype.
05 · Outcomes & Learnings

What I took away

0→1
Built end-to-end without engineering support using AI-assisted prototyping
1
Real user (my daughter) used it to plan her actual summer camps
∞
Confirmed the model: PM who can ship is a different kind of PM

The most important learning wasn't about the product. It was about my process. Building CampSpark in Claude Code forced me to think like an engineer just enough to make better product decisions. I understood the tradeoffs, could scope more precisely, and shipped faster than any traditional PM-to-engineering handoff would have allowed.

"The best PM I can be is one who reduces uncertainty: for users, for engineers, for the business. Building prototypes myself is one of the highest-leverage ways I know to do that."

If I were to take CampSpark further, the next bets would be AI-powered recommendations ("given your child's age and past camps, here are 5 great options") and calendar integration and both natural extensions of the planning-and-coordination thesis.

06 · Why This Matters for My Work

The through-line

CampSpark is a consumer product, but the skills it demonstrates are the same ones that make me effective in B2B: identifying a real pain point, talking to users, making hard scope decisions, and shipping something people actually use.

At Walmart, I built tools for marketplace sellers. At SAP, I owned supplier experience for a platform processing $6 trillion in transactions. In both cases, the core job was the same: reduce cognitive load for busy operators so they can focus on what they're actually good at.

CampSpark is the same problem on a smaller canvas. I built it to stay close to what's actually hard — user research, scope tradeoffs, shipping something that works. That craft doesn't change with the org chart.
