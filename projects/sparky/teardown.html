Product Teardown

Sparky: What Walmart's AI Shopping Assistant Gets Right, and Where It Needs to Go

An evaluation based on structured stress testing across 8 categories: intent, context, accuracy, safety, competition, manipulation, cart actions, and personalization

By Sonal Singh · Principal PM, Walmart Global Tech June 2026
Personal analysis. Opinions are my own, not Walmart's.
TL;DR
What Sparky is
Walmart's AI shopping assistant, embedded in the app and on walmart.com, and also available through ChatGPT and Gemini. It launched publicly in June 2025. It helps shoppers search, compare products, synthesize reviews, and move toward checkout through natural language.
What it gets right
Intent-based product discovery and comparison. When a customer knows roughly what they want, Sparky collapses a 40-result search into 3–5 options with an explanation. In my testing, the core shopping loop worked end to end. Safety and boundary handling were also stronger than expected. Walmart has reported that Sparky users have about 35% higher order value than non-users — a meaningful directional signal, though selection bias likely plays a role.
Where it falls short
Personalization is in-session only. Sparky is transparent about it: it has no access to your purchase history or browsing behavior beyond the current conversation. It also has a meaningful gap in customer-service recovery. When I reported a broken order, it showed no empathy and redirected me to find my own order.
Why it matters
Walmart has the data, the catalog, and the physical store infrastructure that no competitor can match. No competitor appears to have fully solved this in the same omnichannel retail context. Connecting Sparky to that infrastructure is the version of this product that justifies the investment.

The Problem Space

Keyword search is broken for anything that requires judgment. A customer who searches "protein powder for a 45-year-old woman who does not lift weights" is doing something a keyword engine cannot handle well. It returns 200 results. The filters assume you already know what you are filtering for. Most customers do not. They abandon the page or pick something arbitrary.

The shift happening in commerce is from "find a product" to "accomplish a goal." Those are different jobs. Finding a product is a lookup task. Accomplishing a goal requires understanding context, constraints, and tradeoffs. A good conversational AI can collapse a 40-result search into a 3-product recommendation with an explanation. That is a meaningfully faster and more confident path to purchase.

"The job is not 'find running shoes.' The job is 'help me pick one I will actually be happy with, given that I overpronate, have a $120 budget, and need them by Thursday.' Keyword search was never built for that job."

Product Overview

Sparky is Walmart's customer-facing GenAI shopping assistant. It launched publicly in June 2025, embedded in the Walmart app and walmart.com, and extended to ChatGPT and Gemini as part of Walmart's broader push toward agentic shopping. Customers can search in natural language, ask for product comparisons, get help narrowing choices, and add items directly to their cart from the conversation. Sparky is positioned as the front edge of a vision where AI handles more of the shopping journey on the customer's behalf.

Persona 03

Value-conscious shopper, cross-referencing prices. Walmart is one of several tabs open.
Job

Verdict

User Journey and Onboarding

Onboarding is largely invisible. There is no forced tutorial, no setup flow, and no moment where Sparky explains what it can and cannot do. First-time users see a blank conversation window with a placeholder prompt. For customers familiar with ChatGPT, this is frictionless. For many Walmart customers, it is unclear what to type or what to expect.

The aha moment comes when Sparky collapses a vague query into 3–5 relevant products with a plain-language explanation. That is where the value is obvious. The fastest path to that moment is a specific, goal-oriented question. The experience breaks down in three situations: when the user expects personalization based on past purchases, when the user needs help after a broken order, and when the user wants to start a fresh session without knowing if one exists.

Core Features Breakdown

Natural Language Product Search
What it does
Translates conversational queries into catalog results, handling synonyms, attributes, and use-case descriptions that keyword search misses.
Job it serves
Find the right product faster when the customer knows what they need but not exactly what to search for.
Execution
Works well Sparky's most consistent capability. Holds specificity well. Falls short on ambiguous queries where clarifying intent first would improve results.
Product Q&A and Comparison
What it does
Answers specific questions about a product or compares two items side by side in natural language.
Job it serves
Give the Considered Buyer enough information to decide without leaving the page or reading dozens of reviews.
Execution
Works well Strongest use case. In testing, Sparky generated a clean side-by-side comparison table with a humility disclaimer and no hallucinated specs. Quality degrades when catalog attribute data is thin.
Cart Integration
What it does
Adds items directly to cart from Sparky responses; confirms the add and surfaces upsell suggestions.
Job it serves
Collapse the path from intent to cart without leaving the conversation.
Execution
Works well Tested the full loop: "Add the 11-piece utensil set to my cart." Sparky responded confidently ("I've added it") and upsell buttons appeared immediately. Item was confirmed in cart. One caveat: Sparky misidentified the item name in its confirmation ("Farberware 28-piece" vs the actual "11-piece set with holder"), indicating a gap in live cart-state reading.
Boundary and Safety Handling
What it does
Refuses out-of-scope requests (political questions, internal information) and re-anchors the session to shopping.
Job it serves
Protect against misuse while keeping the session productive.
Execution
Works well Refusals were brief and non-preachy in testing. After declining a political question, Sparky immediately re-anchored to the active shopping thread with relevant quick-tap suggestions. Two separate jailbreak attempts were blocked cleanly with different response patterns (identity-reset vs rule-citation).
In-Session Personalization
What it does
Uses information the customer provides during the conversation to refine recommendations within that session.
Job it serves
Make the Efficient Planner feel like Sparky knows them, not just the catalog.
Execution
Partial Sparky is transparent: it has no access to browsing history or purchase history beyond the current conversation. In-session context retention is strong. Cross-session personalization does not exist today. Publicly visible behavior suggests personalization is conversation-driven, not behavior-driven.

Competitive Landscape

Sparky sits in a three-way competitive frame. Amazon's Rufus is the direct analog: a conversational assistant embedded in a major retailer's app, designed for the same shopping jobs. Google AI Mode (Search Labs) approaches from the opposite direction, as a general AI that handles shopping queries with product cards and comparison features. The observations below reflect public behavior and my own testing; they are not based on internal data from any of these companies.

Success Metrics

Measuring a product like Sparky requires three layers. Business metrics tell you if the investment is generating returns. Product metrics tell you if customers find it useful. AI-specific metrics tell you if the model is performing well enough to support the product metrics. Walmart has reported that customers who use Sparky have about 35% higher order value than those who don't. This is a company-reported metric, not an independently verified causal effect. Selection bias likely plays a role: Sparky users may already have higher purchase intent.

Key Additional Findings

Cart-state reading bug. After adding an item ("Add the 11-piece utensil set to my cart"), Sparky confirmed the action but identified the wrong item in its confirmation message, calling it a "Farberware 28-piece" instead of the actual "11-piece set with holder." The item in the cart was correct; the spoken confirmation was not. This points to a disconnect between the cart action and Sparky's live read of cart state.
Customer-service recovery is broken. When I reported a broken order, Sparky showed zero empathy. It told me to find my own order rather than pulling it from my account, and then immediately redirected to suggesting similar replacement products. This is the one clear failure in the entire evaluation. Customers reporting problems need acknowledgment first, not upsells.
Session memory has no controls. Chat history persists within the same app session but is cleared when you kill and relaunch the app. There is no "New Chat" button and no "Clear History" option. This is a gap both for privacy-conscious customers who want a clean start, and for those who want to pick up where they left off across sessions.

Recommendations

Problem
Every session starts cold

A customer who buys organic produce weekly gets the same recommendations as someone who shops for budget groceries. The data to differentiate them already exists in Walmart's CDP; Sparky just cannot see it today.

Recommendation: Connect Sparky's response layer to the CDP's customer profile, starting with category affinity and purchase recency. Gate the rollout to Walmart+ members where data quality is highest. Expected impact: higher add-to-cart rate from Sparky sessions; measurable reduction in "wrong product" returns.
Problem
The post-add-to-cart moment is dead

When a customer adds an item, the conversation ends. There is no follow-up, no "what else do you need today," and no basket-completion prompt. This is the highest-intent moment in the session and Sparky exits it.

Recommendation: Introduce a contextual follow-on prompt after any add-to-cart action, drawing on category adjacencies and past cart composition. A/B test prompt phrasing against a control. Expected impact: 10–15% increase in items per Sparky-assisted session.
Problem
Deal Seekers get nothing from Sparky

No rollback flagging, no price history, no budget-ceiling guidance. This is a large segment of Walmart's customer base and Sparky offers them nothing differentiated from a standard search.

Recommendation: Integrate rollback and price history data into Sparky's response context for products with active promotions. When a customer asks about a product, surface the current rollback status if one exists. Expected impact: improved conversion for price-sensitive customers; a clear differentiator vs Rufus.
Problem
Walmart's omnichannel advantage is invisible inside Sparky

A customer asking about an item available for in-store pickup in two hours gets the same response as one waiting three days for delivery. Sparky does not surface real-time inventory or store proximity proactively.

Recommendation: Use store proximity and live inventory data to inject availability signals into Sparky's recommendations. "Available for pickup today at your local store" should be a default response attribute for any in-stock item within 10 miles. This is Walmart's clearest competitive moat and it is sitting unused.
Problem
Customer-service recovery is broken

When a customer reports a broken order, Sparky shows no empathy, fails to pull the order from their account, and immediately suggests replacement products. This is the experience for a customer who is already frustrated.

Recommendation: Add a customer-service mode that detects frustration signals, acknowledges the problem first, pulls the last relevant order from the account, and offers return or replacement paths before suggesting new products. Expected impact: better post-purchase experience; reduced friction for customers at their most at-risk moment.

What's Next

Sparky's strongest version is a shopping layer that knows who you are, what you care about, and what you probably need next. Walmart has the data, the catalog, and the physical store infrastructure to build that. No competitor appears to have fully solved this in the same omnichannel retail context. The path requires three things moving together: connecting customer history to session context, activating store inventory as a real-time signal in every Sparky response, and building a feedback loop so the model improves from every interaction. None of these is a research problem. They are execution problems, and Walmart is better positioned to solve them than any other retailer operating at this scale.
