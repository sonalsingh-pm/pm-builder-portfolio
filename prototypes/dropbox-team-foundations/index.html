A contractor gets invited to join a client's Dropbox team. She already has a Dropbox account from another client. Dropbox tells her she has to leave that team first. She closes the tab. That's a lost seat.

Dropbox named this problem themselves in this job description: joining a team should feel additive instead of risky, switching between teams should feel natural, admins should understand who has access and why, and licensing should match how teams actually work. None of those things are true today. This brief is about how to fix them.

Five Structural Gaps

Five moments where the product blocks its own growth

Each one is named explicitly in the role description. Each one is a lost seat.

Primary gap

One identity, one team -- forever

Modern work crosses company lines. A freelancer works with three clients. A consultant is embedded in two companies. A contractor moves between projects. Today Dropbox forces a choice: be on one team, or leave it to join another. Every external collaboration invitation that fails to convert is a lost seat.

"Directly caps team expansion at the edges where growth happens most."

Joining a team feels risky

When an invitation arrives, the questions start immediately. What happens to my personal files? Does my existing account change? Will the company own my content? Those questions do not get answered, so people do not join. The anxiety is not about Dropbox -- it is about uncertainty.

"User anxiety at the join moment kills acceptance rates before a seat is ever activated."

Admins don't know who has access or why

Admin visibility into members, external collaborators, pending invitations, and access levels is fragmented. Removing someone creates uncertainty about what they can still see. That uncertainty makes admins hesitant to invite people in the first place.

"Hesitant admins send fewer invitations. Fewer invitations means fewer seats."

Shared credentials are lost revenue

Multiple users sharing one Dropbox account is a known pattern in small teams and creative studios. It hides behind a single login and goes undetected. Progressive interventions that surface this pattern and convert sharers into paying seats are a meaningful growth lever.

"Each converted sharing group is net new revenue -- often multiple seats at once."

Annual seats don't match project-based work

Contractors join for a sprint. Clients need access for a project. Freelancers contribute for a few weeks. An annual seat model forces admins to either over-provision or leave collaborators out entirely. The result is seats that never get bought.

"Flexible licensing captures seats the current model structurally prevents."

What I Would Build

Four fixes. Each one maps to a gap.

Not a full roadmap. A direction.

1

One identity, multiple teams

Let one person participate in multiple Dropbox teams without conflict. One account, clean separation between workspaces, a simple switcher in the nav. The same way you switch between Slack workspaces -- except your files stay where they belong.

2

A join flow that answers the questions before they are asked

Before a user accepts an invitation, show them exactly what changes and what stays the same. Personal files: untouched. Existing teams: unaffected. What they gain: spelled out clearly. When people understand what they are committing to, they commit.

3

An admin view that creates confidence

One screen showing every member, every external collaborator, every pending invitation, and exactly what each person can access. When admins trust what they see, they act on it. More invitations. More seats.

4

Licensing that matches how teams actually work

At Dell, we made this same argument when we launched their first monthly device subscription for SMBs. Small businesses cannot sign a three-year contract when they do not know if they will exist in three years. They need flexibility. Dropbox has the same opportunity. Monthly licensing and simple upgrade paths for external collaborators would capture seats that currently go unbought.

We made the invitation buyer-branded. The supplier saw Dell's name, not SAP's. Suddenly the invitation made sense -- someone they already trusted was asking them to connect. Drop-off fell 73%.

Metrics

How I would measure progress

Six metrics tied directly to the structural gaps. Each one is a signal, not a vanity number.

📨
Team Invitation Acceptance Rate
Invitations sent to seats activated within 7 days. The primary funnel metric -- every percentage point is real revenue.
⌛
Time-to-Join
Invitation sent to first active login. Measures join flow friction. Long gaps mean the join experience is creating doubt.
📬
Admin Invitation Frequency
Invites sent per admin per month. An admin who trusts the system sends more invitations. Trust shows up in behavior.
⇆
Multi-Team Participation Rate
Percentage of users active on more than one team per week. Tracks adoption of the multi-team identity capability.
⬆️
External Collaborator Conversion Rate
External access holders who upgrade to full member seats within 90 days. Measures the external-to-paid upgrade path.
🔑
Password Sharing Intervention Conversion
Flagged shared-credential accounts that convert to separate paid seats. Each conversion is net new revenue.
