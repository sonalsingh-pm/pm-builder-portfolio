Five Structural Gaps

Five moments where the product blocks its own growth

Each one is a structural problem. Each one is fixable.

Primary gap
1
One identity, one team -- forever
Modern work crosses company lines. A freelancer works with three clients. A consultant is embedded in two companies. A contractor moves between projects. Today Dropbox forces a choice: be on one team, or leave it to join another. Every external collaboration invitation that fails to convert is a lost seat.
Directly caps team expansion at the edges where growth happens most.
2
Joining a team feels risky, not additive
When an invitation arrives, the questions start immediately. What happens to my personal files? Does my existing account change? Will the company own my content? Those questions do not get answered, so people do not join. The anxiety is not about Dropbox -- it is about uncertainty.
User anxiety at the join moment kills acceptance rates before a seat is ever activated.
3
Admins don't know who has access or why
Visibility into members, external collaborators, pending invitations, and access levels is fragmented. Removing someone creates uncertainty about what they can still see. That uncertainty makes admins hesitant to invite people in the first place.
Hesitant admins send fewer invitations. Fewer invitations means fewer seats.
4
Shared credentials hide lost revenue
Multiple users sharing one Dropbox account is a known pattern in small teams and creative studios. It hides behind a single login and goes undetected. Progressive interventions that surface this pattern and convert sharers into paying seats represent meaningful growth.
Each converted sharing group is net new revenue -- often multiple seats at once.
5
Annual seats don't match project-based work
Contractors join for a sprint. Clients need access for a project. Freelancers contribute for a few weeks. An annual seat model forces admins to either over-provision or leave collaborators out entirely. The result is seats that never get bought.
Flexible licensing captures seats the current model structurally prevents.

What I Would Build

In more detail

Not a roadmap. A direction.

1
One identity, multiple teams
One Dropbox account should be able to participate in multiple teams simultaneously with clean separation between them. Personal files stay personal. Team files stay in their team. Switch between them from the nav the way you switch between Slack workspaces. No forced choices. No separate accounts.
2
A join flow that removes uncertainty
Before a user accepts an invitation, show them exactly what changes and what stays the same. Personal files: untouched. Existing teams: unaffected. What they gain: spelled out clearly. When people understand what they are committing to, they commit. Acceptance rate goes up.
3
Admin visibility that builds confidence
One screen. Every member, every external collaborator (people with folder access but not full team members -- they do not consume a paid seat), every pending invitation. Each row shows how someone joined, when they were last active, and what happens if you remove them. When that clarity exists, admins invite more people.
4
Licensing that matches how teams actually work
At Dell, we made this exact argument when we launched their first monthly device subscription for SMBs. Small businesses cannot sign a three-year contract when they do not know if they will exist in three years. They need flexibility. Monthly licenses and simple upgrade paths for external collaborators would capture seats that the annual model structurally prevents.

Metrics

How I would measure progress

Six metrics tied directly to the structural gaps. Each one is a signal, not a vanity number.

📨
Team Invitation Acceptance Rate
Invitations sent to seats activated within 7 days. The primary funnel metric -- every percentage point is real revenue.
⏱
Time-to-Join
Invitation sent to first active login. Measures join flow friction. Long gaps mean the experience is creating doubt.
📬
Admin Invitation Frequency
Invites sent per admin per month. An admin who trusts the system sends more invitations. Trust shows up in behavior.
🔀
Multi-Team Participation Rate
Percentage of users active on more than one team per week. Tracks adoption of the multi-team identity capability.
⬆️
External Collaborator Conversion Rate
External access holders who upgrade to full member seats within 90 days. Measures the external-to-paid upgrade path.
🔑
Password Sharing Intervention Conversion
Flagged shared-credential accounts that convert to separate paid seats. Each conversion is net new revenue.
