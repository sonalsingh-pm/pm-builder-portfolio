Lumen: The Missing Layer in Walmart's Customer Growth Engine

A behavioral signals decisioning layer, and what it would take to build it inside Walmart

By Sonal Singh · Principal PM May 2026
Speculative work, not affiliated with Walmart
Executive Summary
The problem
Walmart has behavioral data on 270 million weekly customers. The gap is not collection. It is the layer that turns existing signals into the right action, channel, and message for each specific person.
What Lumen is
A decisioning layer between Walmart's behavioral data and its customer channels. It scores propensity, selects action and channel, generates the message, and closes a feedback loop.
Where to start
Walmart+ at-risk retention. High LTV, clear intervention window, labeled outcome data to train on, and a baseline to beat.

The Problem

Walmart collects behavioral data at scale: 270 million customers every week across app, web, and 4,700 stores.1 Early results from Sparky, Walmart's AI shopping assistant, show what acting on these signals can unlock: users build 35% larger baskets than non-users.2 The data is not the constraint. What happens next is.

Three gaps frame the opportunity. The data gap is smallest: Walmart already captures signals at scale. The identity gap is real: in-store shoppers without a linked app account generate no addressable record, and Walmart has not disclosed what share of its weekly visitors are identifiable digitally.3 The decisioning gap is the actual opportunity: for the customers Walmart can already identify, behavioral signals are not consistently becoming individual-level action. That is what Lumen addresses. The identity gap is covered separately below.

"For the customers Walmart can identify, the gap is not data. It is decisioning: knowing which signal matters, which action to take, and which channel to use, for that specific person."

Where the Gap Shows Up

01
Price Sensitivity Without Detection

A customer browses electronics for 20 minutes, adds three items to the cart, and leaves. Walmart knows the event. It does not know why. A decisioning layer that infers price sensitivity from browse-to-abandon patterns sends a targeted offer to that customer. Blanketing everyone with discounts trains customers to wait for them.

02
Decision Fatigue Without Curation

A customer searches "running shoes," browses 40 product pages, adds two pairs to the cart, and leaves. They are not indifferent. They are overwhelmed. The right response is a short curated list that collapses the decision. Most systems respond with more product exposure, which makes it worse.

Lumen: The Decisioning Layer

Lumen reads behavioral signals, scores propensity, selects the right action and channel, generates the message, and logs the outcome. Signal velocity matters as much as signal presence: a customer trending from weekly to monthly visits is a different risk than one who has always been monthly. Every outcome feeds back into the model without manual tuning.

Principle 1
Individual over segment

Two customers in the same segment can receive different recommendations if their signals diverge. Lumen decides at the person level, not the cohort level.

Principle 2
Channel matching is a first-class output

A push notification to a desktop-primary shopper does not land. Lumen always pairs an action recommendation with a channel recommendation, based on each customer's engagement history.

Principle 3
The loop must close

A decisioning layer that does not learn from outcomes is a rules engine with extra steps. Every outcome Lumen generates becomes a training signal. That is the difference between a campaign tool and a growth engine.

How This Would Ship at Walmart

Where it lives
Customer Growth team, under CRM and Personalization. Intersects AI/ML personalization, experimentation, customer data intelligence, and lifecycle engagement.
Systems it connects to
Customer Data Platform, marketing automation (email, push, SMS), homepage personalization, Walmart+ membership data, and the A/B experimentation platform.
Data it needs
Individual behavioral events, channel engagement signals, Walmart+ renewal dates and benefit usage, and labeled outcomes: who churned, purchased again, or upgraded.
Teams it depends on
CDP (event ingestion, identity resolution), ML Science (model training), MarTech (channel execution), App and Web (surface rendering), Legal and Privacy (consent).

Three-Phase Rollout

Phase 1: Months 1–4
Walmart+ At-Risk Retention
Primary metric: 30-day cancellation rate Depends on: CDP, Walmart+ data, email and push Risk: Model accuracy without sufficient labeled data

Target Walmart+ members showing disengagement within 60 days of renewal. One propensity model, one experiment: does a personalized message beat the standard renewal reminder?

Phase 2: Months 5–10
Cart Recovery and New Customer Activation
Primary metrics: Cart recovery rate, second-purchase rate Depends on: Session-level event streaming, A/B infra at scale Risk: Overlap with existing marketing automation

Add cart recovery (browse-to-abandon signals) and new customer activation. The key challenge is ensuring Lumen's decisions do not duplicate or conflict with existing campaign logic.

Phase 3: Months 11–18
Full Lifecycle Decisioning
Primary metrics: 90-day LTV, cross-category purchase rate Depends on: Real-time event streaming, full channel integration Risk: Model drift at scale, governance of automated decisions

Decisioning expands across the full lifecycle: activation, engagement, lapsed recovery, and upgrade nudges. The model retrains weekly on labeled outcomes from every action Lumen takes.

Solving In-Store Identity

In-store drives over 80% of Walmart's sales.1 Most of those transactions are anonymous. Three practical approaches to close the gap, each at a different friction level:

1
Phone number at checkout
Bridges anonymous shoppers to a behavioral profile. The prompt already exists at many stores. The incentive needs to be stronger: a coupon converts better than "get your receipt by text."
2
Scan & Go as behavior capture
Every Scan & Go session is an authenticated app user. The goal is to make it the default path for regular shoppers, not a buried optional feature.
3
Walmart Pay as the identity bridge
An app user who pays in-store with a credit or debit card still generates an anonymous transaction. Walmart Pay links that purchase to their account, connecting in-store behavior to a known digital profile.

How Success Gets Measured

Metrics are defined before experiments run. Primary metrics drive go/no-go decisions. Secondary metrics explain the mechanism. The guardrail prevents discount conditioning.

Primary
30-day W+ cancellation rate
Do at-risk members cancel less when they receive a personalized message vs. the standard renewal reminder?
Primary
Cart recovery rate
Of high-intent abandoners who receive a re-engagement, what percent purchase within 7 days?
Primary
Second-purchase rate
Among first-time buyers who receive an activation nudge, what percent return within 21 days?
Secondary
Cross-category purchase rate
Do channel-matched recommendations lead to expansion into new categories within 30 days?
Secondary
Session frequency
Change in weekly sessions per customer in the 30 days after a personalized recommendation.
Guardrail
Promo redemption rate
Must not rise more than 5% above baseline. Conversion driven only by discounts means the model is training the wrong behavior.

The Prototype

I built a proof of concept rather than just describing the idea. Select a customer scenario and the decisioning layer outputs a propensity score, next-best action, recommended channel, timing, and a sample message. The demo uses preloaded data and is intentionally simplified.

Honest Limitations

This is speculative work built without access to Walmart's data or systems.

  • Propensity scores are directionally reasonable but not calibrated. Real scores require labeled outcome data: who churned, who upgraded, and what signals preceded each.
  • The feedback loop is described, not implemented. Closing it in production requires a real pipeline: action logged, outcome labeled, model retrained on cadence.
