Lumen: The Missing Layer in Walmart's Customer Growth Engine

A behavioral signals decisioning layer, and what it would take to build it inside Walmart

By Sonal Singh · Principal PM May 2026
Speculative work, not affiliated with Walmart
Executive Summary
The problem
Walmart generates behavioral data on 270 million weekly customers. The opportunity is not more data collection. It is the layer that turns existing signals into the right action, the right channel, and the right message for each specific person, not a segment they happen to belong to.
What Lumen is
A decisioning layer between Walmart's behavioral data and its customer-facing channels. It reads individual signals, scores propensity, selects the best action and channel, generates the message, and closes a feedback loop so the system improves from outcomes over time.
Where to start
Walmart+ at-risk retention. High LTV, defined intervention window, enough labeled outcome data to train a propensity model, and a clear baseline to beat.

The Problem

Walmart is the largest retailer on earth. 270 million customers visit every week, generating behavioral data across app, web, and 4,700 stores.1 John Furner, Walmart's President and CEO, has spoken about building more personalized and frictionless shopping experiences. Early results from Sparky, Walmart's AI shopping assistant, point in the right direction: customers who use it build 35% larger baskets than those who do not.3

Three distinct gaps exist. The data gap is the smallest: Walmart already collects behavioral signals at scale. The identity gap is real but tractable: customers who shop only in-store generate no addressable customer record, and Walmart does not disclose what share of its 270 million weekly visitors have a linked digital profile.7 The decisioning gap is where the opportunity lives: for the customers Walmart can identify, behavioral signals are not consistently translating into individual-level action. Lumen addresses this third gap. The identity gap is addressed separately below.

"For the customers Walmart can identify, the gap is not data. It is decisioning: knowing which signal matters, which action to take, and which channel to use, for that specific person."

Where the Gap Shows Up

01
Price Sensitivity Without Detection

A customer browses electronics for 20 minutes, adds three items to the cart, and abandons. Walmart knows the event. It does not know why. A decisioning layer that infers price sensitivity from browse-to-abandon patterns sends a targeted offer to the right customer, not everyone. Blanketing discounts trains customers to wait for them.

02
Decision Fatigue Without Curation

A customer who searches "running shoes," browses 40 product pages, adds two pairs to the cart, and leaves is not indifferent. They are overwhelmed. The right response is a "we picked three for you" module that collapses the decision space. Most systems respond with more product exposure, which makes this worse.

Lumen: The Decisioning Layer

Lumen sits between Walmart's behavioral data and its customer-facing channels. For each identified customer, it reads behavioral signals, scores propensity, selects the right action and channel, generates the message, and logs the outcome. A key input is not just what a customer's signals look like today but how they are changing: a customer trending from weekly to monthly purchases is a different churn risk than one who has always been monthly. Every outcome feeds back into the model. The system improves without manual tuning.

Principle 1
Individual over segment

Lumen makes decisions at the individual level. Two customers in the same segment can receive different recommendations if their signals point in different directions.

Principle 2
Channel matching is a first-class output

The right message on the wrong channel is not delivered. A push notification to a desktop-primary shopper is ignored. Lumen always includes a channel recommendation with a reason.

Principle 3
The loop must close

A decisioning layer that does not learn is a rules engine with extra steps. Every outcome Lumen generates feeds back into the model. That is the difference between a campaign tool and a closed-loop growth engine.

How This Would Ship at Walmart

Lumen connects systems Walmart already has. The PM owns the product spec and experiment roadmap. Engineering owns the data pipeline. Data Science owns the propensity models.

Where it lives in the org
Customer Growth team, under the CRM and Personalization function. Sits at the intersection of AI/ML personalization, experimentation, customer data intelligence, and lifecycle engagement.
Systems it connects to
Customer Data Platform (behavioral events, purchase history, app engagement), marketing automation (email, push, SMS), homepage personalization engine, Walmart+ membership data, and the A/B experimentation platform.
Data it needs
Individual-level behavioral events from app and web, channel engagement signals (email open rates, push opt-in status), Walmart+ renewal date and benefit usage, and labeled outcome data: who churned, who purchased again, who upgraded.
Teams it depends on
CDP and Data Platform (event ingestion and identity resolution), ML Science (propensity model training), MarTech (channel execution and suppression rules), App and Web (surface rendering), Legal and Privacy (consent and data use).

Three-Phase Rollout

Phase 1: Months 1–4
Walmart+ At-Risk Retention
Primary metric: 30-day cancellation rate Depends on: CDP, Walmart+ data, email and push Risk: Model accuracy without sufficient labeled data

One use case: Walmart+ members showing disengagement signals within 60 days of renewal. Build one propensity model. Run one experiment. Prove that a personalized retention message beats the standard renewal reminder. This validates the infrastructure that everything else depends on.

Phase 2: Months 5–10
Cart Recovery and New Customer Activation
Primary metrics: Cart recovery rate, second-purchase rate Depends on: Session-level event streaming, A/B infra at scale Risk: Overlap with existing marketing automation

Two additional use cases: cart recovery using browse-to-abandon signals, and new customer activation. The focus shifts from proving infrastructure to improving model quality and ensuring Lumen's decisions do not conflict with existing campaign logic.

Phase 3: Months 11–18
Full Lifecycle Decisioning
Primary metrics: 90-day LTV, cross-category purchase rate Depends on: Real-time event streaming, full channel integration Risk: Model drift at scale, governance of automated decisions

Decisioning extends across the full customer lifecycle: acquisition-to-activation, engagement deepening, lapsed recovery, and Walmart+ upgrade nudges. The feedback loop is now operational: every action generates a labeled outcome and the model retrains weekly.

Solving In-Store Identity

In-store shopping drives over 80% of Walmart's sales.1 The identity gap is a product problem: transaction data exists, but it sits behind anonymous transactions. Three practical approaches, in order of least to most friction:

1
Phone number at self-checkout for digital receipt
The prompt already exists at many locations. The gap is the incentive. "Get your receipt by text" does not convert. "Get a $2 coupon for your next visit" does. Requires MarTech integration to link the number to a behavioral profile before the customer leaves the store.
2
Scan & Go as the identity capture mechanism
Every Scan & Go session is an authenticated app user. Make it the default path for regular shoppers, not an optional feature buried in the app. Works best in high-traffic urban stores first.
3
Walmart Pay as the identity bridge
Every Walmart Pay transaction is already linked to a known account. Increasing adoption among existing app users partially closes the identity gap without building anything new. Does not reach customers who do not have the app.

How Success Gets Measured

Metrics are defined before the experiment runs. The primary metric is the go/no-go decision. Secondary metrics explain the mechanism. The guardrail ensures conversion is driven by genuine engagement, not discount conditioning.

Primary
30-day W+ cancellation rate
Do at-risk members who receive a personalized recommendation cancel less than those who receive the standard renewal flow?
Primary
Cart recovery rate
Of high-intent abandoners who receive a personalized re-engagement, what percent complete a purchase within 7 days?
Primary
Second-purchase rate
Among first-time buyers who receive a personalized activation nudge, what percent make a second purchase within 21 days?
Secondary
Cross-category purchase rate
Do customers who receive channel-matched recommendations expand into new categories within 30 days?
Secondary
Session frequency
Change in weekly sessions per customer in the 30 days following a personalized recommendation.
Guardrail
Promo redemption rate
Must not rise more than 5% relative to baseline. Conversion driven only by discounts means the model is training the wrong behavior.

Honest Limitations

This is speculative work built without access to Walmart's data or systems. Two material gaps apply to the prototype:

  • Propensity scores shown are directionally reasonable but not calibrated. Real scores require labeled outcome data: who actually churned, who actually upgraded, and what signals preceded each.
  • The feedback loop is described, not implemented. Productionizing it requires a real pipeline: action logged, outcome labeled, model retrained on cadence. The prototype demonstrates the concept; it does not close the loop.
