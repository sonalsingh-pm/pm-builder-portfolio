Lumen: The Missing Layer in Walmart's Customer Growth Engine

A behavioral signals decisioning layer, and what it would take to build it inside Walmart

By Sonal Singh · Principal PM May 2026
Speculative work, not affiliated with Walmart
Executive Summary
The problem
Most retail personalization at scale is segment-based. Customers are grouped into buckets, and the bucket determines the action. The problem with segments is that they flatten the variation that matters most. A price-sensitive cart abandoner and a decision-fatigued one can look identical in aggregate. They need completely different responses. Walmart generates behavioral signals on 270 million weekly customers across app, web, and 4,700 stores. The question is whether those signals are reaching the right action, on the right channel, at the right moment, for each specific person, or whether they are being averaged into a segment response that misses too many of them.
The hypothesis
A decisioning layer that reads individual signals, scores propensity, selects the right channel and timing, and closes a feedback loop on outcomes would produce meaningfully better results than segment-based rules. This concept demonstrates what that layer could look like.
What Lumen is
A concept for the layer between Walmart's behavioral data and its customer-facing channels. It reads signals per customer, scores propensity, selects the best action and channel, and generates the message at the individual level.
What makes it different
Individual-level decisions rather than segment rules. Channel selection as a first-class output, not an afterthought. A feedback loop that retrains on outcomes rather than requiring manual tuning.
Where to start
Walmart+ at-risk retention: clear intervention window, measurable outcome, high LTV stakes, and enough labeled outcome data to train a propensity model quickly. This validates the core infrastructure before expanding to cart recovery and new-customer activation.
How to measure success
Three use cases, each with a primary metric: 30-day cancellation rate for at-risk Walmart+ members; cart recovery rate for high-intent abandoners; second-purchase rate for new customers within 21 days. One guardrail across all three: promo redemption rate must not rise, because discounts are not the goal.

The Problem

Walmart is the largest retailer on earth. 270 million customers visit every week.1 They generate behavioral data across app, web, and 4,700 physical stores: what people search, browse, add to cart, abandon, buy, and return. CEO Doug McMillon has named personalization as one of three strategic AI priorities.2 Walmart is building a system to give every customer a unique homepage. Sparky, Walmart's AI shopping assistant, is an early signal of what is possible: customers who interact with it build 35% larger baskets than those who do not.3

And yet the gap is visible. Industry research finds 80% of consumers are more likely to purchase from brands that offer personalized experiences.5 And yet 92% of retailers say they already personalize effectively, while only 48% of consumers agree.6 E-commerce cart abandonment averages above 70% industry-wide;4 Walmart does not publicly disclose its own rate. Walmart has noted that 100+ million weekly shoppers engage with digital tools in stores,7 but a significant share of its 270 million weekly visitors remain digitally unidentified: Walmart cannot reach them between visits, cannot predict when they will stop coming, and cannot invite them to Walmart+.

The gap is not data. Walmart has the data. The gap is the layer between knowing something about a customer and doing something about it: the right action, the right channel, the right moment, for that specific person. Most retail personalization at this scale has not fully made that transition from segment-based rules to individual-level decisioning. That is the gap Lumen is designed to address.

"The gap is not data. Walmart has the data. The gap is the layer between knowing something and doing something about it."

Three Places Walmart Loses Customers It Could Keep

01
Price Sensitivity Without Detection

A customer browses electronics for 20 minutes, adds three items to the cart, and abandons. Walmart knows the event. It does not know why. Was it price? Shipping cost? A competitor? Without that interpretation, the follow-up is a generic "you left something behind" email that converts at 2-3%. A decisioning layer that infers price sensitivity from browse-to-abandon patterns sends a targeted promotion to the right customer, not everyone. Blanketing discounts trains customers to wait for them.

02
Decision Fatigue Without Curation

Walmart.com has tens of millions of SKUs. A customer who searches "running shoes," browses 40 product pages, adds two pairs to the cart, and leaves is not indifferent. They are overwhelmed. The right response is curation: a "we picked three for you" module that collapses the decision space. Most platforms respond with more product exposure. That is the wrong move, and the data shows it in session abandonment rates.

03
The Unidentified In-Store Customer

In-store shopping still drives over 80% of Walmart's sales.1 A customer who shops in-store twice a week, spends $150 per visit, and has never touched the app is invisible to every personalization system. Walmart cannot promote to them, cannot predict churn, cannot convert them to Walmart+. This is the identity resolution problem, and the paths that solve it (email capture, phone number at checkout, app download) are underutilized. This is not a data science gap. It is a product gap.

What Makes Lumen Different

Most personalization systems at retailers operate on segments. A customer falls into a bucket ("lapsed grocery buyer," "high-AOV at-risk") and the bucket determines the action. Segments are a useful starting point, but they collapse the variance that matters most.

Two customers can share identical RFM scores and need completely different interventions. A price-sensitive customer near Walmart+ renewal gets a different recommendation than a price-sensitive customer who has never heard of Walmart+. Segment-level systems cannot distinguish them. Individual-level decisioning can.

Principle 1
Individual over segment

Lumen reads eight signals per customer and interprets them in combination. The decision is made at the individual level, not mapped to a pre-defined bucket.

Principle 2
Channel matching is a first-class output

The right message on the wrong channel is not delivered. A push notification to a desktop-primary shopper is ignored. An email to an app-only user sits unread. Lumen's output always includes a channel recommendation with a reason. The prototype renders what the touchpoint looks like in Walmart's actual UI.

Principle 3
The loop must close

A decisioning layer that does not learn is a rules engine with extra steps. Every action taken by Lumen generates a signal. Every signal feeds back into the model. Over time the system learns which recommendations work for which customers and refines future decisions automatically. That is the difference between a campaign tool and a growth engine.

The Signal Framework

The foundation is RFM: Recency, Frequency, Monetary value. Every serious growth team at Walmart, Amazon, and Target uses it. It is not novel. It is the baseline that any personalization system must start from.

DimensionWhat it measuresHigh signalLow signal
RecencyHow recently purchasedLast 7 days90+ days
FrequencyHow often they purchaseWeeklyQuarterly or less
MonetaryHow much they spend$150+ per orderUnder $30

RFM alone is not enough. What matters is trajectory, not snapshot. A customer who was weekly and is now monthly is a different problem than one who has always been monthly. The decisioning layer must read the direction of change, not just the current position.

How This Would Ship at Walmart

The decisioning layer is not a standalone product. It is infrastructure that sits between existing systems. Here is how it would actually get built.

Where it lives in the org
Customer Growth team, sitting under Walmart Connect or the broader CRM and Personalization function. PM owns the product spec and experiment roadmap. Engineering owns the pipeline. Data Science owns the propensity models.
Systems it connects to
Customer Data Platform (behavioral events, purchase history, app engagement), marketing automation (email, push, SMS), homepage personalization engine, Walmart+ membership data, and the experimentation platform.
Data it needs
Individual-level behavioral events (browse, cart, purchase, return), channel engagement signals (email opens, push opt-in, app vs. web session share), Walmart+ status and renewal date, and labeled outcome data (who churned, who upgraded).
Teams it depends on
CDP / Data Platform (event ingestion), ML Science (propensity models), MarTech (channel execution), App and Web (surface rendering), Legal and Privacy (consent and data use), and Marketing Operations (message QA and compliance).

Three-Phase Rollout Plan

Start narrow, validate fast, expand with evidence. The worst mistake is building a full decisioning platform before proving that individual-level recommendations outperform segment-level ones.

Phase 1: Months 1–4
Walmart+ At-Risk Retention
Primary metric: 30-day cancellation rate Depends on: CDP, Walmart+ data, email/push channels Risk: Model accuracy without sufficient labeled data

Focus entirely on one use case: members showing early disengagement signals (declining session frequency, reduced purchases, low benefit usage) who are within 60 days of renewal. The business case is clear: Walmart+ LTV is high, churn is costly, and the intervention window is defined. Build one propensity model. Run one experiment. Prove that personalized outreach beats the standard renewal reminder.

This phase validates the core infrastructure: can we identify the right customers, select the right action, deliver on the right channel, and measure the outcome? Everything else depends on this working.

Phase 2: Months 5–10
Cart Recovery and New Customer Activation
Primary metrics: Cart recovery rate, 2nd purchase rate Depends on: Session-level event streaming, A/B infra at scale Risk: Channel saturation if recommendations overlap with existing campaigns

Expand to two additional use cases. Cart recovery: use browse-to-abandon signals to infer the reason (price sensitivity, decision fatigue, friction) and send a differentiated response, not a generic reminder. New customer activation: identify the moment when a first-time buyer is most likely to purchase again and intervene with a curated recommendation in their top category.

By this point the infrastructure is proven. The focus shifts to model quality and channel coordination: making sure the decisioning layer does not conflict with existing marketing automation rules.

Phase 3: Months 11–18
Full Lifecycle Decisioning
Primary metrics: 90-day LTV, cross-category purchase rate Depends on: Real-time event streaming, full channel integration Risk: Model drift at scale, governance of automated decisions

Extend decisioning across the full customer lifecycle: acquisition-to-activation, engagement deepening, lapsed customer recovery, and Walmart+ upgrade nudges. At this stage the feedback loop is operational: every action taken generates a labeled outcome, the model retrains on a weekly cadence, and recommendation quality improves without manual intervention.

The goal of Phase 3 is not a wider campaign. It is a self-improving system that gets more accurate over time and requires less manual tuning to operate.

Solving In-Store Identity

The in-store customer problem is a product problem, not a data science problem. The data exists (store visit frequency, category spend, transaction size) but it is locked behind anonymous transactions. The work is building the product flows that capture consent-based identity without creating friction that breaks the checkout experience.

Five concrete approaches, in order of least to most friction:

1
Phone number at self-checkout for digital receipt
The prompt already exists at many Walmart locations. The gap is the incentive. "Get your receipt by text" is weak. "Get a $2 coupon for your next visit" converts.
Tradeoff: Requires MarTech integration to link the phone number to a behavioral profile before the customer leaves the store.
2
Scan & Go as the app download driver
Position Scan & Go not as a convenience feature but as the identity capture mechanism. Every Scan & Go session is an authenticated app user. The in-store UX should make Scan & Go the default path for regular shoppers.
Tradeoff: Requires associate training and store-level rollout. Works best in high-traffic urban stores first.
3
Walmart Pay as the identity bridge
Every Walmart Pay transaction is already linked to a known account. Increase Walmart Pay adoption among existing app users and the in-store identity problem partially solves itself. No new product required, just better merchandising of an existing feature.
Tradeoff: Does not capture customers who do not have the app. Solves the activation gap, not the acquisition gap.
4
Associate-initiated identity capture
At high-spend checkout lanes, train associates to offer app signup with an immediate $5 credit. This targets the customers most worth capturing (high AOV, frequent shoppers) and converts them at the highest-value moment.
Tradeoff: Associate compliance is hard to enforce at scale. Works as a piloted program in specific store formats before broader rollout.
5
Fuel station loyalty card with phone number
Walmart fuel centers have a captive audience waiting at the pump. A "save on fuel with your phone number" prompt captures identity with a clear immediate benefit (gas savings) without requiring app download as a prerequisite.
Tradeoff: Fuel station coverage is not universal. Works in markets where Walmart fuel stations have high usage.

How Success Gets Measured

Metrics must be chosen before the experiment runs, not after. The primary metric drives the go/no-go decision. Secondary metrics explain the mechanism. Guardrails exist to prevent optimizing one number at the expense of something that matters.

Primary
30-day W+ cancellation rate
Measures whether at-risk members who receive a personalized recommendation are less likely to cancel than those who receive the standard renewal flow.
Primary
Cart recovery rate
Of customers who abandon a cart and receive a personalized re-engagement, what percent complete a purchase within 7 days.
Primary
2nd purchase rate (new customers)
Among first-time buyers who receive a personalized activation nudge, the rate of a second purchase within 21 days.
Secondary
Cross-category purchase rate
Whether customers who receive channel-matched recommendations expand into new categories within 30 days.
Secondary
Session frequency
Change in weekly app or web sessions per customer in the 30 days following a personalized recommendation.
Guardrail
Promo redemption rate
Must not increase beyond 5% relative to baseline. If customers are converting only because of discounts, the model is training the wrong behavior.
Example Experiment: Walmart+ At-Risk Retention
Hypothesis
Sending a personalized savings summary to at-risk Walmart+ members 14 days before renewal will reduce 30-day cancellation rate compared to the standard renewal reminder.
Control
Standard Walmart+ renewal reminder email (current production experience).
Variant
Personalized email showing: total dollars saved via Walmart+ this year, number of free deliveries used, exclusive member deals accessed, and a curated recommendation in their top purchase category.
Target segment
Walmart+ members within 14–21 days of renewal who show at least two disengagement signals: session frequency decline >30%, no purchase in 45+ days, or Walmart+ benefit usage below their 6-month average.
Primary metric
30-day cancellation rate
Guardrail
Promo redemption rate. Unsubscribe rate must not exceed 2x baseline.
Decision rule
If cancellation rate drops by >5% relative lift at 95% statistical confidence after 21 days, ship to all at-risk members. If not significant after 28 days, do not ship. Investigate message quality or segment targeting.
Sample size
Approximately 12,000 per variant, assuming 8% baseline cancellation rate and 10% minimum detectable effect at 95% confidence.

Honest Limitations

This is speculative work. The signal inputs are simplified. The AI responses come from a general-purpose language model, not a propensity model trained on Walmart's first-party outcome data. The recommendations would be materially better with real data and real feedback loops.

  • The eight dropdown fields are a proxy for a real CDP feed. A production system reads continuous event streams, not manually entered values.
  • Propensity scores from an LLM are directionally reasonable but not calibrated. Real scores require training on labeled outcome data: who actually churned, who actually upgraded.
  • The feedback loop is described, not implemented. Productionizing it requires a real data pipeline: action logged, outcome labeled, model retrained on cadence.
  • Channel recommendations do not account for suppression rules, frequency caps, or opt-out status, all of which a production system must respect.
With access to real data and systems, I would:
  1. Replace dropdown inputs with a real-time CDP feed so the system analyzes actual customer profiles, not manually constructed scenarios.
  2. Train propensity models on historical outcome data (actual churn events, actual Walmart+ upgrades) and backtest against held-out periods.
  3. Build the feedback loop as a production data pipeline: action taken, outcome recorded within 30 days, model retrained weekly.
  4. Run the Walmart+ at-risk retention experiment described above and use the results to validate whether the decisioning layer beats the baseline before expanding scope.

Why I Built This

I applied for the Principal PM role on Walmart's Customer Growth team because the problem is genuinely hard. Not technically. The technology exists. Hard in a product sense: knowing which signal matters, which intervention to run first, how to design a system that gets smarter without constant manual tuning.

My background is in B2B platforms and fulfillment, not consumer growth. I built Lumen to close that gap: to think through the problem from first principles and build something I could demonstrate rather than just describe.
