Lumen: The Missing Layer in Walmart's Customer Growth Engine

A behavioral signals decisioning layer, and what it would take to build it inside Walmart

By Sonal Singh · Principal PM May 2026
Speculative work, not affiliated with Walmart
Executive Summary
The problem
Walmart generates behavioral data on 270 million weekly customers. The opportunity is not more data collection. It is the layer that turns existing signals into the right action, the right channel, and the right message for each specific person, not a segment they happen to belong to.
What Lumen is
A decisioning layer between Walmart's behavioral data and its customer-facing channels. It reads individual signals, scores propensity, selects the best action and channel, generates the message, and closes a feedback loop so the system improves from outcomes over time.
Where to start
Walmart+ at-risk retention. High LTV, defined intervention window, enough labeled outcome data to train a propensity model, and a clear baseline to beat. Validating this use case proves the infrastructure before expanding scope.

The Problem

Walmart is the largest retailer on earth. 270 million customers visit every week.1 They generate behavioral data across app, web, and 4,700 stores: what they search, browse, add to cart, abandon, buy, and return. John Furner, Walmart's President and CEO, has spoken publicly about building more personalized and frictionless shopping experiences for customers.2 Early results from Sparky, Walmart's AI shopping assistant, are encouraging: customers who use it build 35% larger baskets than those who do not.3

Three distinct gaps exist. The data gap is the smallest: Walmart already collects behavioral signals at scale. The identity gap is real but solvable: customers who shop only in-store generate no addressable customer record. Walmart+ membership stands at approximately 28 million8 out of 270 million weekly visitors, and Walmart does not disclose how many of the rest have a linked digital profile. The decisioning gap is where the opportunity lives: for the customers Walmart can identify, behavioral signals are not always translating into individual-level action. Lumen addresses this third gap. The identity gap is addressed separately below.

Two customers with the same purchase profile can need completely different responses. A price-sensitive cart abandoner needs a targeted promotion. A decision-fatigued one needs curation, not more product exposure. Segment-level systems cannot tell them apart. Individual-level decisioning can.

"For the customers Walmart can identify, the gap is not data. It is decisioning: knowing which signal matters, which action to take, and which channel to use, for that specific person."

Where the Gap Shows Up

01
Price Sensitivity Without Detection

A customer browses electronics for 20 minutes, adds three items to the cart, and abandons. Walmart knows the event. It does not know why. Was it price? Shipping cost? A competitor? Without that interpretation, the follow-up is a generic reminder that converts at 2-3%. A decisioning layer that infers price sensitivity from browse-to-abandon patterns sends a targeted promotion to the right customer, not everyone. Blanketing discounts trains customers to wait for them.

02
Decision Fatigue Without Curation

Walmart.com has tens of millions of SKUs. A customer who searches "running shoes," browses 40 product pages, adds two pairs to the cart, and leaves is not indifferent. They are overwhelmed. The right response is curation: a "we picked three for you" module that collapses the decision space. Most systems respond with more product exposure. That is the wrong move, and it shows in session abandonment rates.

03
The Unidentified In-Store Customer

In-store shopping drives over 80% of Walmart's sales.1 A customer who shops twice a week, spends $150 per visit, and has never used the app is invisible to every personalization system. Walmart cannot promote to them, cannot predict churn, and cannot invite them to Walmart+. This is the identity gap. The approaches to closing it are product decisions, not data science ones, and they are addressed below.

Lumen: The Decisioning Layer

Lumen sits between Walmart's behavioral data and its customer-facing channels. For each identified customer, it reads behavioral signals, scores propensity, selects the right action and channel, generates the message, and logs the outcome. A key input is not just what a customer's signals look like today but how they are changing: a customer trending from weekly to monthly purchases is a different churn risk than one who has always been monthly. Every logged outcome feeds back into the model. The system improves without manual tuning.

Principle 1
Individual over segment

Lumen makes decisions at the individual level, not the segment level. Two customers in the same bucket can receive different recommendations if their signals point in different directions.

Principle 2
Channel matching is a first-class output

The right message on the wrong channel is not delivered. A push notification to a desktop-primary shopper is ignored. An email to an app-only user sits unread. Lumen always includes a channel recommendation with a reason.

Principle 3
The loop must close

A decisioning layer that does not learn is a rules engine with extra steps. Every action Lumen takes generates an outcome. Every outcome feeds back into the model. That is the difference between a campaign tool and a closed-loop growth engine.

How This Would Ship at Walmart

Lumen connects systems Walmart already has. The PM owns the product spec and experiment roadmap. Engineering owns the data pipeline. Data Science owns the propensity models.

Where it lives in the org
Customer Growth team, under the CRM and Personalization function. Sits at the intersection of the four disciplines in the team's charter: AI/ML personalization, experimentation, customer data intelligence, and lifecycle engagement.
Systems it connects to
Customer Data Platform (behavioral events, purchase history, app engagement), marketing automation (email, push, SMS), homepage personalization engine, Walmart+ membership data, and the A/B experimentation platform.
Data it needs
Individual-level behavioral events from app and web (browse, cart, purchase, return), channel engagement signals (email open rates, push opt-in status), Walmart+ renewal date and benefit usage, and labeled outcome data: who churned, who purchased again, who upgraded.
Teams it depends on
CDP and Data Platform (event ingestion and identity resolution), ML Science (propensity model training and evaluation), MarTech (channel execution and suppression rules), App and Web (surface rendering), Legal and Privacy (consent and data use governance).

Three-Phase Rollout

Start narrow. Prove the core claim before expanding scope: that individual-level recommendations outperform segment-level ones. Each phase adds a use case only after the previous one is validated.

Phase 1: Months 1–4
Walmart+ At-Risk Retention
Primary metric: 30-day cancellation rate Depends on: CDP, Walmart+ data, email and push channels Risk: Model accuracy without sufficient labeled data

One use case: Walmart+ members showing disengagement signals (session frequency decline, no purchase in 45+ days, low benefit usage) who are within 60 days of renewal. The business case is clear: LTV is high, churn is costly, and the intervention window is defined. Build one propensity model. Run one experiment. Prove that a personalized retention message beats the standard renewal reminder. This phase validates the infrastructure that everything else depends on.

Phase 2: Months 5–10
Cart Recovery and New Customer Activation
Primary metrics: Cart recovery rate, second-purchase rate Depends on: Session-level event streaming, A/B infra at scale Risk: Recommendation overlap with existing marketing automation

Two additional use cases. Cart recovery: use browse-to-abandon signals to infer the reason (price sensitivity, decision fatigue, or friction) and send a differentiated response, not a generic reminder. New customer activation: identify when a first-time buyer is most likely to return and send a curated recommendation in their top category. The focus shifts from proving infrastructure to improving model quality and ensuring Lumen's decisions do not conflict with existing campaign logic.

Phase 3: Months 11–18
Full Lifecycle Decisioning
Primary metrics: 90-day LTV, cross-category purchase rate Depends on: Real-time event streaming, full channel integration Risk: Model drift at scale, governance of automated decisions

Extend decisioning across the full customer lifecycle: acquisition-to-activation, engagement deepening, lapsed recovery, and Walmart+ upgrade nudges. The feedback loop is now operational: every action generates a labeled outcome and the model retrains weekly. The goal is a self-improving system, not a wider campaign.

Solving In-Store Identity

The identity gap is a product problem. The data exists (transaction records, category spend, visit frequency) but it sits behind anonymous transactions. The work is building flows that capture consent-based identity without adding friction at checkout. Five approaches, in order of least to most friction:

1
Phone number at self-checkout for digital receipt
The prompt exists at many locations. The gap is the incentive: "Get your receipt by text" does not convert. "Get a $2 coupon for your next visit" does.
Tradeoff: Requires MarTech integration to link the phone number to a behavioral profile before the customer leaves the store.
2
Scan & Go as the identity capture mechanism
Every Scan & Go session is an authenticated app user. Make it the default path for regular shoppers, not an optional feature buried in the app.
Tradeoff: Requires associate training and store-level rollout. Works best in high-traffic urban stores first.
3
Walmart Pay as the identity bridge
Every Walmart Pay transaction is already linked to a known account. Increasing adoption among existing app users partially solves the identity problem without building anything new.
Tradeoff: Captures existing app users only. Does not close the acquisition gap for customers who do not have the app.
4
Associate-initiated identity capture
At high-spend checkout lanes, associates offer app signup with a $5 credit. Targets the most valuable customers at the moment of highest engagement.
Tradeoff: Associate compliance is hard to enforce at scale. Best piloted in specific store formats before broader rollout.
5
Fuel station identity capture
Walmart fuel centers have a captive audience. "Save on fuel with your phone number" captures identity with a clear immediate benefit, no app download required.
Tradeoff: Fuel station coverage is not universal. Most effective in high-usage markets.

How Success Gets Measured

Metrics are chosen before the experiment runs. The primary metric is the go/no-go decision. Secondary metrics explain the mechanism. The guardrail prevents optimizing for discounts rather than genuine engagement.

Primary
30-day W+ cancellation rate
Do at-risk members who receive a personalized recommendation cancel less than those who receive the standard renewal flow?
Primary
Cart recovery rate
Of high-intent abandoners who receive a personalized re-engagement, what percent complete a purchase within 7 days?
Primary
Second-purchase rate
Among first-time buyers who receive a personalized activation nudge, what percent make a second purchase within 21 days?
Secondary
Cross-category purchase rate
Do customers who receive channel-matched recommendations expand into new categories within 30 days?
Secondary
Session frequency
Change in weekly sessions per customer in the 30 days following a personalized recommendation.
Guardrail
Promo redemption rate
Must not rise more than 5% relative to baseline. Conversion driven only by discounts means the model is training the wrong behavior.
Example Experiment: Walmart+ At-Risk Retention
Hypothesis
A personalized savings summary sent 14 days before renewal will reduce 30-day cancellation rate for at-risk members compared to the standard renewal reminder.
Control
Standard Walmart+ renewal reminder email (current production experience).
Variant
Personalized email showing total dollars saved via Walmart+ this year, free deliveries used, member deals accessed, and a curated recommendation in their top purchase category.
Target segment
Walmart+ members within 14–21 days of renewal showing at least two disengagement signals: session frequency decline >30%, no purchase in 45+ days, or benefit usage below their 6-month average.
Primary metric
30-day cancellation rate
Guardrail
Promo redemption rate. Unsubscribe rate must not exceed 2x baseline.
Decision rule
If cancellation rate drops by >5% relative lift at 95% confidence after 21 days, ship to all at-risk members. If not significant after 28 days, do not ship. Investigate message or targeting.
Sample size
Approximately 12,000 per variant, assuming 8% baseline cancellation rate and 10% minimum detectable effect at 95% confidence.

Honest Limitations

This is speculative work. The signal inputs are simplified. The AI responses come from a general-purpose language model, not a propensity model trained on Walmart's first-party data. The recommendations would be materially better with real data and real feedback loops.

  • The dropdown fields are a proxy for a real CDP event stream. A production system reads continuous behavioral events, not manually entered values.
  • Propensity scores from an LLM are directionally reasonable but not calibrated. Real scores require labeled outcome data: who actually churned, who actually upgraded.
  • The feedback loop is described, not implemented. Productionizing it requires a real pipeline: action logged, outcome labeled, model retrained on cadence.
  • Channel recommendations do not account for suppression rules, frequency caps, or opt-out status, all of which a production system must respect.
With access to real data and systems, I would:
  1. Replace dropdown inputs with a real-time CDP feed so the system analyzes actual customer profiles, not manually constructed scenarios.
  2. Train propensity models on historical outcome data (actual churn events, actual Walmart+ upgrades) and backtest against held-out periods.
  3. Build the feedback loop as a production data pipeline: action taken, outcome recorded within 30 days, model retrained weekly.
  4. Run the Walmart+ at-risk retention experiment and use the results to validate whether the decisioning layer beats the baseline before expanding scope.
