# SKILL: Writing a One-Page Problem Brief

## Purpose
The problem brief is the foundation of every project in this portfolio. It is written before the PRD and answers one question with ruthless clarity: is this problem worth solving?

A world class brief is not a PRD in disguise. It is short, sharp, and honest. It should take 20 minutes to write and 5 minutes to read. If it takes longer to read, it is too long.

---

## Before Writing — Ask These Questions

Never start a brief without answers to these. If the person cannot answer them, help them think through the answers first.

1. **What is the specific painful moment?** Not "users struggle with X" but the exact moment where the pain is felt — "I spent three weekends searching for camps and still made the wrong choice."
2. **Who specifically feels this pain?** Name a specific person — not "busy professionals" but "dual-income parents with school-age kids who both work full time."
3. **What do they do today?** Walk through the exact manual steps they take to solve this without your product.
4. **What is the cost of the current approach?** Time, money, stress, missed outcomes — be specific.
5. **What is the early validation signal?** Personal experience, organic workarounds others adopted, analogous markets, interviews. Be honest about what is confirmed vs. assumed.
6. **Why now?** What has changed recently that makes this more urgent or more solvable?

---

## Structure — One Page, Six Sections

### 1. The Problem (2-3 sentences)
State the problem in plain human language. Write it the way the user would describe it — not the way a PM would. No product language. No solution language.

**What makes it world class:**
- Specific and vivid — the reader should feel the pain
- Focuses on the human cost, not the technical gap
- Written from the user's perspective

**Common mistakes:**
- Describing the solution ("there is no platform that...")
- Too abstract ("users face challenges with...")
- Mixing problem and solution

### 2. Who Has It (2-3 sentences)
Name the specific person who feels this pain most acutely. Include enough context that the reader can picture a real human being.

**What makes it world class:**
- Specific demographic and behavioral context
- States why this person feels it more than others
- Does not try to include everyone

### 3. Current Workaround (bullet list)
Walk through exactly what this person does today. Step by step. Show the manual, broken, painful process.

**What makes it world class:**
- Specific steps — not "they search online" but "they visit 10-15 individual camp websites and manually track options in a spreadsheet"
- Shows the friction at each step
- Makes the reader think "there has to be a better way"

### 4. Cost of the Problem (2-3 sentences or bullets)
What does it cost the user to live with this problem? Be specific — hours per week, dollars, missed outcomes, emotional cost.

**What makes it world class:**
- Quantified where possible ("several hours across multiple weekends")
- Includes both practical and emotional cost
- Makes the reader feel the urgency

### 5. Early Signal (2-3 sentences)
What evidence exists that this problem is real and that a solution would be adopted? Personal experience counts. Organic workarounds others adopted count. Be honest about confidence level.

**What makes it world class:**
- Specific and honest — not "research shows" without a source
- Organic adoption signals are the strongest ("two other parents used my Google Sheet without being asked")
- States confidence level clearly — validated vs. assumed

### 6. Why Now (2-3 sentences)
What has changed recently that makes this problem more urgent or more solvable? Macro trends, technology shifts, behavioral changes.

**What makes it world class:**
- Names specific, recent changes — not just "the market is growing"
- Explains why this moment specifically is the right time
- If there is no good answer to "why now" — flag this as a risk

---

## Format

Output as clean Markdown. Target length: 400-600 words. Never more than one page when printed.

Use this exact structure:

```markdown
# [Problem Name] — Problem Brief

## The Problem
[2-3 sentences]

## Who Has It
[2-3 sentences]

## Current Workaround
- [Step 1]
- [Step 2]
- [Step 3]
...

## Cost of the Problem
[2-3 sentences or bullets]

## Early Signal
[2-3 sentences]

## Why Now
[2-3 sentences]

---
*Brief authored by Sonal Singh | [Date]*
```

---

## Quality Checklist

Before delivering a brief, verify:
- [ ] Problem is stated without solution language
- [ ] A specific person is named — not a broad demographic
- [ ] Current workaround is step by step — not vague
- [ ] Cost is quantified — not just "it's frustrating"
- [ ] Early signal is honest about confidence level
- [ ] Why now names specific recent changes
- [ ] Total length is under 600 words
- [ ] Reads like a human wrote it — not a PM template

---

## What a World Class Brief Unlocks

A great brief makes the PRD easier to write, the prototype easier to scope, and the case study easier to tell. Every subsequent artifact should be traceable back to the brief. If the PRD solves a different problem than the brief describes — something went wrong.
